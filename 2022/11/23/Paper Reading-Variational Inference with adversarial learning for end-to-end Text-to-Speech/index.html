Variational Inference with adversarial learning for end-to-end Text-to-Speech

VITS (Variational Inference with adversarial learning for end-to-end Text-to-Speech) is a highly expressive speech synthesis model that combines variational inference, normalizing flows, and adversarial training. Unlike traditional methods that directly concatenate spectral features in speech synthesis, VITS connects the acoustic model and the vocoder using latent variables. It models randomness on the latent variables and utilizes a stochastic duration predictor to enhance the diversity of synthesized speech. With VITS, given the same input text, it can generate speech with different tones and rhythms, providing greater flexibility and naturalness in speech synthesis.

Proposed Approach

VITS mainly consists of three components:

Conditional Variational AutoEncoder (VAE)
Alignment estimates generated from variational inference
Generative adversarial training

Variational Inference

The generator of VITS can be viewed as maximizing the variational lower bound, also known as the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO), of a conditional Variational AutoEncoder (VAE).

  1. Reconstruction Loss
    During training, VITS still generates Mel-spectrograms to guide the model training. The target sample points in the reconstruction loss are Mel-spectrograms rather than the original waveform:

$$
L_{recon}=||x_{mel}-\hat{x}_{mel}||_1
$$

However, during inference, there is no need to generate Mel-spectrograms. Mel-spectrograms are only used to compute this reconstruction loss.

  1. KL Divergence
    The input to the prior encoder includes phonemes generated from the text and the alignment between phonemes and latent variables . The alignment matrix is of size , representing the strictly monotonic attention matrix for each phoneme’s duration. Therefore, the KL divergence is:

$$
L_{kl}=log\ q_{\phi}(z|x_{lin})-log\ p_{\theta}(z|c_{text},A)
$$

where represents the posterior distribution of outputting latent variables given the linear spectrogram , and represents the prior distribution of outputting latent variables given the condition . The latent variable is sampled from:

$$
z\sim q_{\phi}(z|x_{lin})=N(z;\mu_{\phi}(x_{lin}),\sigma_{\phi}(x_{lin}))
$$

To provide higher resolution information to the posterior encoder, linear spectrograms rather than Mel-spectrograms are used as the input to the posterior encoder . Additionally, to generate more realistic samples, it’s crucial to enhance the expressive power of the prior distribution. Therefore, normalizing flows are introduced to enable reversible transformations between the simple distribution generated by the text encoder and the complex distribution corresponding to latent variables . After the up-sampled encoder output, a series of reversible transformations are applied:
$$
p_{\theta}(z|c)=N(f_{\theta}(z);\mu_{\theta}(c),\sigma_{\theta}(c))|det\frac{\partial f_{\theta}(z)}{\partial z} |
$$

Here, the input is the up-sampled encoder output:

$$
c=[c_{text},A]
$$

Alignment Estimation

Since there are no real “alignment” labels during training, it is necessary to estimate the alignment between text and audio at each iteration during training.

  1. Monotonic Alignment Search
    To estimate the alignment between text and speech, VITS employs a method similar to Monotonic Alignment Search (MAS) used in Glow-TTS. This method aims to find an optimal alignment path to maximize the log-likelihood of the data parameterized by the normalization flow :

$$
A=\mathop{argmax}\limits{\hat{A}}\mathop{log}p(x|c_{text},\hat{A})\ =\mathop{argmax}\limits{\hat{A}}\mathop{log}N(f(x);\mu(c_{text},\hat{A}),\sigma(c_{text},\hat{A}))
$$

MAS Constraints: The optimal alignment obtained through MAS must be monotonic and non-skipping. However, directly applying MAS to VITS is not feasible because VITS optimizes the ELBO rather than the log-likelihood of deterministic latent variables . Therefore, a slight modification to MAS is made to find the optimal alignment path that maximizes the ELBO:
$$
\mathop{argmax}\limits{\hat{A}}\mathop{log}p_{\theta}(x_{mel}|z)-\mathop{log}\frac{q_\phi(z|x_{lin})}{p_\theta(z|c_{text},\hat{A})}=\mathop{argmax}\limits{\hat{A}}\mathop{log}p_\theta(z|c_{text},\hat{A})=\mathop{log}N(f_{\theta}(z);\mu_{\theta}(c_{text},\hat{A}),\sigma_{\theta}(c_{text},\hat{A}))
$$

Predicting Duration from Text

The stochastic duration predictor is a flow-based generative model that introduces random variables and with the same time resolution and dimensionality as the duration sequence. It samples these two variables using the approximate posterior distribution . The training objective is the variational lower bound of the phoneme duration log-likelihood:
$$
\mathop{log}p\theta(d|c_{text})\geq \mathbb{E}{q{\phi}(u,v|d,c_{text})}[\mathop{log}\frac{p_{\theta}(d-u,v|c_{text})}{q_{\phi}(u,v|d,c_{text})}]
$$

During training, the gradients of the stochastic duration predictor are disconnected to prevent them from affecting other modules. Phoneme durations are sampled from random noise through the reversible transformation of the stochastic duration predictor and then converted into integer values.

Adversarial Training

The discriminator is introduced to distinguish whether the output is generated by the decoder or real waveform . VITS employs two types of loss functions. The first one is the least-squares loss function used for adversarial training:
$$
L_{adv}(D)=\mathbb{E}_{(y,z)}[(D(y)-1)^2+(D(G(z)))^2]
$$

$$
L_{adv}(G)=\mathbb{E}_z[(D(G(z))-1)^2]
$$

The second type is the feature-matching loss, specifically applied to the generator:

$$
L_{fm}(G)=\mathbb{E}{(y,z)}[\sum{l=1}^{T}\frac{1}{N_l}||D^l(y)-D^l(G(z))||_1]
$$

Where represents the number of layers in the discriminator, denotes the output feature map of the -th layer of the discriminator, and represents the number of feature maps. The feature-matching loss can be considered as a reconstruction loss, used to constrain the output of intermediate layers in the discriminator.

Loss

VITS can be seen as a joint training of VAE and GAN. Therefore, the overall loss is given by:

$$
L_{vae}=L_{recon}+L_{kl}+L_{dur}+L_{adv}+L_{fm}(G)
$$

Structure

The overall structure of VITS can be divided into 5 components:

Prior Encoder: Text Encoder + Normalizing Flow to enhance the complexity of the prior distribution. When applied to multi-speaker models, speaker embedding vectors are added to the residual modules of the normalizing flow.

Decoder: Essentially the generator of the vocoder HiFi-GAN V1. When applied to multi-speaker models, a linear layer is added after the speaker embedding vectors, which are then concatenated to the output latent variables of .

Stochastic Duration Predictor: Estimates the distribution of phoneme durations from conditional input . When applied to multi-speaker models, a linear layer is added after the speaker embedding vectors, which are then concatenated to the output of the text encoder.

Posterior Encoder: Takes linear spectrograms as input during training and outputs latent variables , while during inference, latent variables are generated by . The posterior encoder of VITS adopts the non-causal WaveNet residual modules used in WaveGlow and Glow-TTS. When applied to multi-speaker models, speaker embedding vectors are added to the residual modules. Used for training only.

Discriminator: Essentially the multi-scale discriminator of HiFi-GAN. Used for training only.

Summary

In summary, VITS is an end-to-end speech synthesis model that directly maps characters or phonemes to waveforms. The model adopts a generative adversarial training mode, with its generator consisting of multiple modules based on normalizing flows. VITS has a large model size and exhibits excellent synthesis quality.

While the idea behind VITS is insightful, understanding its workings can be challenging, especially regarding the normalizing flows component. For more information on normalizing flows, you can refer toAwesome Normalizing Flows。
